\section*{Cancer Genetics}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Important role of somatic mutations and epigenetic changes
	\item Cancer progression resembles Darwinian evolution
	\item Conflict between selection at the level of the organism vs. its constitutent cells
\end{itemize}
At the same time, cancer provides many lessons on the different ways in which mutations can disrupt gene function, and how dominance or recessiveness can arise. These principles are generally applicable to other traits as well.

\subsection*{Cancer is a Heterogeneous Group of Disorders}
But they share common properties:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Uncontrolled cell proliferation
    \item Cell spreading
    \begin{itemize}
        \item invasion of adjacent tissues
        \item migration to other parts of the body through blood and lymph systems
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{``Tumor Progression''}
The disease phenotype involves a progressive conversion of the descendants of a normal cell into malignant cancer cells
\begin{center} 
	\includegraphics*[width=0.8\textwidth]{W13_1.png} 
\end{center}

\subsection*{Progression from Normal Cells to Cancer Cells Stage Nomenclature and Properties}
\textbf{Growth stages:}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Hyperplastic growth:} excessive number of cells that appear the same as those in normal tissue
	\item \textbf{Dysplastic growth:} a growth that has cytologically abnormal cells
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Tumor stages:}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Benign tumor:} 
	\begin{itemize}
        \item no invasion of neighboring tissue (but can disrupt organ function)
        \item self-limiting, grow slowly
        \item can be removed surgically with low risk of recurrence
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Malignant tumor} (commonly called ``cancer'' or ``neoplasm''):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item tumor cells spread to neighboring or distant tissues (“metastasis”)
        \item grow rapidly
        \item high risk of recurrence after removal
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Darwinian Model of Tumor Progression}
Analogous to natural selection.  Selection within the body for cells with genetic advantages in:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Proliferation
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Independence from growth signals
        \item Immunity to signals that inhibit growth
    \end{itemize}
    \item Survival: immunity to extrinsic and intrinsic death signals
    \item Competition: for nutrients and oxygen (vascularization)
    \item Migration: to colonize new sites (metastasis)
\end{itemize}
Steps of developing cancer:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Somatic mutation (can include primary epigenetic ``mutations'' because they can be inherited through somatic cell divisions)
	\item Genetic heterogenity
	\item Some mutant cells have advantage
	\item Expansion of mutant clone
	\item Go back to (1)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Consequences of Darwinian Model of Tumor Progression}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Clonality:} Tumors descend from a single ancestral cell that becomes cancerous
	\item \textbf{Genetic Heterogeneity} of tumor cells
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Early evidence of clonality}
In a given female tumor, the cancer cells all express alleles from the same X chromosome.

\subsection*{Principle: Mutations that Impair DNA Repair and Genome Stability Facilitate Darwinian Cancer Progression}
The somatic mutation stage (from above) is enhanced by mutations that block repair of DNA damage, block post-replication repair of mismatched bases, or reduce genome stability.
\begin{itemize}
	\item All types of mutation (and thus all repair and genome stability pathways) can be relevant
\end{itemize}
This includes:
\begin{itemize}
	\item point mutations
	\item repeat expansions
	\item deletions
	\item inversions
	\item translocations
	\item aneuploidies
	\item epimutations
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Dominance and Recessiveness}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Gain of function mutations are usually dominant
	\item Loss of function mutations are usually recessive
	\item Human cells are diploid.
\end{itemize}
Considering the ``Darwinian Model'', would you expect a cancer-promoting somatic mutation to be dominant, recessive, or either?  Recall the Darwinian Model:
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Somatic mutation (can include primary epigenetic ``mutations'' because they can be inherited through somatic cell divisions)
	\item Genetic heterogenity
	\item Some mutant cells have advantage
	\item Expansion of mutant clone
	\item Go back to (1)
\end{enumerate}
They should be \textbf{dominant}.  Otherwise, they will have no advantage!  But dominant in what sense?
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Dominant/recessive when we are thinking about \underline{biochemical processes} in the \textbf{individual cells} of an organism
	\item Dominant/recessive when we are thinking about \underline{inheritance} of the \textbf{organismal} phenotype
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Examples}
\textbf{Biochemical basis of albinism:}
\begin{itemize}
	\item A dominant allele (C/C or C/c) would produce a functional tyrosinase which converts tyrosine to melanin.  This results in pigmented cells.
	\item C is dominant, c is recessive.  A recessive c/c would not produce the tyrosinase enzyme, therefore pigment is missing.
	\item Because the albino allele (c) does not make the functional enzyme whereas the wild type does, loss-of-function alleles are expected to be recessive to their wildtype.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Biochemical basis of Pseudoachondroplasia (Dwarfism)}
\begin{itemize}
	\item A recessive allele (p/p) would result in normal bone growth.
	\item A dominant allele (P/p or P/P) would cause an inhibitor to cell division to be produced, with or without the right signal, resulting in abnormal (reduced) bone growth.
	\item Because the pseudoachondroplasia allele (P) encodes a hyperactive protein, gain-of-function alleles are expected to be dominant to their wildtype counterparts.
\end{itemize}
In these examples, Dominant/recessive patterns of \textbf{inheritance} coincide with Dominance and Recessiveness at the \textbf{cellular level}\dots because the organismal phenotype depends on all (or most) relevant cells having the same phenotype.
\begin{itemize}
	\item For this to occur, fertilization must have generated that genotype in the zygote.  
	\item \textbf{But there are cases where they don't coincide, especially for cancer.}
\end{itemize}

\section*{The Paradox of Tumor Suppressor Genes}
\subsection*{Few Familial Cancers are due to Inheritance of Proto-oncogene Mutations}
Many proto-oncogene mutations are known in \textbf{Sporadic} cancers
\begin{itemize}
	\item e.g., \textit{TGF$\alpha$, FGFR, Ras, myc, MLL, cyclins, Cdks, Bcl-2} and many more
\end{itemize}
However, few familial cancers syndromes are due to inheritance of proto-oncogene mutations
\begin{itemize}
	\item MEN2 (Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia Type 2):
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Thyroid, Parathyroid, and Adrenal tumors
        \item \textit{RET} proto-oncogene, a tyrosine kinase
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Methods which Loss-of-Function Mutations can Occur in Tumor Suppressor Genes}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Complete or partial deletions
	\item Small or large insertions at many possible positions within the gene
    \item Chromosomal rearrangements with breakpoints at many possible positions within the gene
    \item Frameshift mutations at many possible positions
    \item Nonsense mutations at many possible positions
    \item Missense mutations at many possible positions
\end{itemize}
But because recessive, somatic mutation would NOT confer an immediate proliferation advantage (heterozygous cells)
\begin{itemize}
	\item By the same token, they could be inherited through germline and cause recessive familial cancer syndromes
\end{itemize}
Are there any sporadic cancers due to somatic loss-of-function mutations in tumor suppressor genes?
\begin{itemize}
	\item Yes, many!
\end{itemize}
Are there many heritable cancer syndromes due to germline loss-of- function mutations in tumor suppressor genes?
\begin{itemize}
	\item Yes, many!
\end{itemize}
Are they recessive?
\begin{itemize}
	\item No!
	\item They are all inherited as dominant traits.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Are These Mutations Really Dominant?}
\textbf{Heterokaryon Tests} for dominance at the \textbf{cellular} level
\begin{center} 
	\includegraphics*[width=0.6\textwidth]{W13_14.png} 
\end{center}
\begin{itemize}
	\item If heterokaryon forms tumor: the cancer alleles are \textbf{dominant}
	\item If heterokaryon does not form tumor: the cancer alleles are \textbf{recessive}
\end{itemize}
Heterokaryon results: These mutations are \textbf{recessive} at the cellular level.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Why are the resulting familial cancer syndromes inherited as dominant?
	\item In sporadic cancers, how can an initial  loss-of-function mutation confer an advantage?
\end{itemize}
Explanation: \textbf{The Two-Hit Mechanism and Loss of Heterozygosity}

\section*{The Two-Hit Mechanism and Loss of Heterozygosity}
Recall the two presentations of retinoblastoma from earlier.  The image of retinoblastoma.
\begin{itemize}
	\item 60\% is unilateral (single tumor in single eye)
	\item 40\% is bilateral (multiple tumors in both eyes)
	\item Only the bilateral one is heritable.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Loss of Heterozygosity explains Dominant Inheritance of Cancer}
For cancer caused by loss-of-function \textit{Rb} Mutations:
\begin{itemize}
	\item An individual who inherits a loss-of-function allele is heterozygous and has controlled cell division.
	\item But only one mutation event is then needed to lose \textit{Rb} function completely in a cell (``loss of heterozygosity'')
	\item That cell can then give rise to a tumor.
	
    \item Probability of that new mutation in \textit{any one cell}: very low.
    \item Probability in \textit{at least one cell} among many in the retina: \textbf{virtually 100\%}
\end{itemize}
So \textit{Rb$^-$} is recessive at the \textbf{cellular} level, but dominant at the \textbf{organismal} level.

\subsection*{Quantitative Evidence: Studies by Alfred Knudsen (1970s)}
\begin{center} 
	\includegraphics*[width=0.7\textwidth]{W13_15.png} 
\end{center}

\subsection*{Loss of Heterozygosity and Tumor Risk}
Loss of heterozygosity also explains difference in tumor risk and tumor number.\\\\
\textbf{Mutations in somatic cells} are inherited by somatic daughter cells.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Ex. Unilateral retinoblastoma
	\item Every cell in the organism starts out \textit{Rb} $+/+$
	\item Cell that initiates the cancer is \textit{Rb} $-/-$
	\item Requires two somatic mutation events (rare)
	\item Results in one tumor in one eye
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Mutations in germline cells} are inherited by subsequent generations
\begin{itemize}
	\item Ex. bilateral retinoblastoma
	\item Every cell in the organism starts out \textit{Rb} $-/+$
	\item Cell that initiates the cancer is \textit{Rb} $-/-$
	\item Requires only \textbf{one somatic mutation event} (not rare)
	\item Results in multiple tumors in both eyes
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{This is why Familial Retinoblastoma is a General Cancer Susceptibility Syndrome}
Only one hit is required to produce cancer in other tissues, too.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Incidence of non-retinal tumors increases with age in retinoblastoma patients.
	\item Around 40 years after retinoblastoma diagnoses, patients have an approximately 30\% chance of also being diagnosed with some other form of cancer.
\end{itemize}

	\item Deletion
	\item Chromosomal rearrangement (where one breakpoint inactivates \textit{Rb})
	\item Aneuploidy (loss of the chromosome with the WT allele)
	\item Epigenetic silencing
	\item \textbf{Mitotic recombination in somatic cells}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Other Mechanisms of Dominance by Loss-of-Function Mutations in Tumor Suppressors}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Dominant-negative} effects by some loss-of-function mutations
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Mutant protein interferes with function of wild-type protein in heterozygous cells
    \end{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Haploinsufficiency} of some tumor suppressor genes
	\begin{itemize}
        \item One wild type copy is not enough for normal cell behavior
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
For example: \textbf{Li-Fraumeni Syndrome}.  General cancer susceptibility due to loss-of-function mutations in tumor suppressor \textit{p53}.  Like Retinoblastoma, it is inherited as \textit{autosomal dominant}.

\subsection*{Loss of Heterozygosity is NOT the whole story for \textit{p53}}
Many observations are consistent with p53 as a tumor suppressor (and the two-hit hypothesis to explain dominance of \textit{null} p53 mutations).  BUT:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Unlike \textit{Rb}, most \textit{p53} mutations in cancer are \textbf{missense} alleles, rather than frameshift or nonsense alleles.  (i.e., most are not likely to be null)
	\item Missense-mutant \textit{p53$^*$} cDNAs \textit{increase} the oncogenic effects of \textit{ras} overexpression when introduced into \textit{p53$^+$} / \textit{p53$^+$} cells: they \textbf{interfere} with the wild-type \textit{p53} alleles.
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Explanation:} Most \textit{p53} mutations in cancers are \textit{dominant negative} missense mutations.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Dominant negative} effects arise when the normal protein functions as part of a multi-subunit complex, and a mutant subunit compromises the function of the entire complex.
\end{itemize}
\begin{center} 
	\includegraphics*[width=0.9\textwidth]{W13_20.png} 
\end{center}
Dominant-negative alleles \textbf{antagonize} the function of the wild type allele

\subsection*{Haploinsufficiency}
Haploinsufficiency is another cause of dominance by loss-of-function mutations.
\begin{itemize}
	\item Occurs in cases where one wild-type alleles does not provide enough gene product for full function.
	\item Some examples:
	\begin{itemize}
        \item \emph{p27/Kip1} tumor suppressor.  Inhibitor of CDKs
        \item \emph{Smad4} tumor suppressor.  Txn factor: negatively regulates growth of epithelial cells
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Cancer Genetics Summary}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Proto-oncogenes
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Normal function is to promote proliferation or survival (regulated)
        \item Gain of function mutations cause cancer
        \item Dominant at cell and organismal levels
    \end{itemize}
	\item Tumor suppressor genes
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Normal function is to block proliferation or survival of cells
        \item Loss of function mutations cause cancer
        \item Dominant at organismal level (recessive or dominant at cell level)
    \end{itemize}
	\item Reasons for dominance of oncogenic loss-of-function mutations
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Loss of heterozygosity (organismal level)
        \item Dominant negative alleles (cell and organismal level)
        \item Haploinsufficiency (cell and organismal level)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section*{Fighting Cancer}
\subsection*{Traditional Strategies}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Surgery - only for solid tumors; difficult if metastasized.  ($< 100\%$ effective)
	\item Radiation Therapy - localized X-rays to kill cancer cells.  (side effects)
	\item Chemotherapy - cytotoxic drugs that kill dividing cells  (side effects)
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Challenges}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Effectiveness: Stop cancer from \textbf{relapsing}
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Incomplete killing / removal of cancer cells
        \item development of resistance to drug treatment (adaptation, mutation)
        \item new cancer mutations induced by therapy itself
    \end{itemize}
    \item Side effects: \textbf{must selectively target} only cancer cells and not normal cells
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Newer Cancer Therapy Approaches}
Ameliorate but don't eliminate side effects and improve efficiency
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Targeted Chemotherapy - inhibit \textbf{specific oncogene} products responsible for the \textbf{specific cancer}
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Angiogenesis inhibitors (bevacizumab); signal transduction inhibitors (imatinib)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Monoclonal Antibody therapies (mAb):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Inhibit cancer cell multiplication / survival
        \item Push cancer cells to commit apoptosis
        \item Stimulate immune system-mediated cancer killing
        \item Directly kill cells by delivering toxins / radiolabels
    \end{itemize}
    \item Stimulate body's own defenses
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Target immune checkpoint modulators (PD-L1 mark, CTLA-4)
        \item Activate apoptosis (small molecule inhibitors of IAPs)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Adoptive cell transfer: genetically engineered T cells
    \begin{itemize}
        \item CARs, TCRs, and TILs
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Targeted Cancer Drug Therapies}
Drugs or biomolecules that fight cancer by interfering with specific \textbf{molecular targets} (e.g., \textbf{oncogene} proteins or mRNAs) involved in the growth, progression, and spread of cancer.\\\\
Differ from standard chemotherapy in several ways:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Act on specific molecular targets} that are associated with cancer.  Most standard chemotherapies act on \textbf{all} rapidly dividing cells (normal and cancerous).
	\item \textbf{Deliberately chosen or designed} to interact with their target.  Many standard chemotherapies simply kill any dividing cells.
	\item Targeted therapies are often \textbf{cytostatic} (block cell proliferation).  Standard chemotherapy agents are \textit{cytotoxic}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Choosing Targets: Proto-oncogenes or Tumor Suppressors?}
Generally, proto-oncogenes, because inhibitors of the protein function (or RNA interference to block expression) should slow cancer.  But also suitable for tumor suppressors if the cancer-causing mutation is dominant negative: use RNA interference to block expression of the MUTANT protein
 
\subsection*{How are Targeted Therapies Developed?}
Most targeted therapies are either small molecules or monoclonal antibodies.
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textcolor{red}{Small molecule compounds ($\cdot$)} are typically developed for targets located inside the cell, because such agents can enter cells relatively easily.
    \item \textcolor{blue}{Monoclonal antibodies (Y)} are relatively large and cannot enter cells, so they are used for targets that are outside cells or on the cell surface.
\end{itemize}
Monoclonal antibodies are obtained by immunizing mice.  Before use in humans, they must be “humanized” by replacing as much of the mouse antibody protein as possible with corresponding portions of human antibodies.

\subsection*{How are the Targets Chosen?}
\textbf{Approach 1:} Proteins that are expressed at higher levels in cancer cells compared to normal cells, especially if they are known to be involved in cell division or survival.\\\\
An example: Human epidermal growth factor Receptor 2 (HER-2)
\begin{itemize}
	\item Targeted by: Herceptin a monoclonal antibody that binds to HER2, blocking the receptor and recruiting immune system cells to kill them.
\end{itemize}
Only approved to treat certain \textbf{breast} and \textbf{stomach} cancers \textbf{that overexpress \textit{HER-2}}
\begin{center} 
	\includegraphics*[width=0.6\textwidth]{W13_22.png} 
\end{center}
\textbf{Approach 2:} Oncogenes activated by \textbf{missense mutations}.\\\\
An example: Oncogene BRAF (mutant allele V600E), a signaling cascade kinase
\begin{itemize}
	\item Mutation present in many melanomas
	\item Targeted by Vemurafenib (Zelboraf), which blocks the kinase activity of this mutant protein and may stop growth of the cancer cells.
	\item Only approved to treat patients with \textbf{inoperable} or \textbf{metastatic} melanoma \textbf{with this particular BRAF mutation}
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Approach 3:} Oncogenes activated as fusion genes by \textbf{chromosome rearrangements}\\\\
An example: BCR-ABL fusion oncogene, a signaling cascade tyrosine kinase
\begin{itemize}
	\item Targeted by imatinib mesylate (Gleevec), a small molecule that blocks the tyrosine kinase activity of the protein made by the \textit{bcr/abl} fusion oncogene.
	\item Only approved for \textbf{chronic myelogenous leukemia}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Summary of Available Types of Targeted Cancer Therapy}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Immunotherapies trigger the immune system to destroy cancer cells.
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Monoclonal antibodies to cell surface target molecules
        \item Monoclonal antibodies that bind to immune system cells to make them more effective killers
        \item Monoclonal antibodies that deliver toxic molecules
    \end{itemize}
    \item Signal transduction inhibitors block the activities of molecules that participate in mitogen signaling cascades.
    \item Gene expression modulators modify the function of proteins that play a role in controlling gene expression.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Also: siRNAs, antisense oligonucleotides and PNAs to block expression directly
    \end{itemize}
    \item Apoptosis inducers cause cancer cells to undergo controlled cell death.
    \item Angiogenesis inhibitors block the growth of new blood vessels to tumors, e.g inhibitors of vascular epithelial growth factor (VEGF)
    \item Hormone therapies stop the growth of hormone-dependent tumors by inhibiting hormone production
    \begin{itemize}
        \item e.g breast cancer (estrogen); prostate cancer (androgen)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Limitations of Targeted Cancer Therapies}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Difficult to develop drugs for some identified targets because of the target's structure and/or the way its function is regulated in the cell.\\
    Example: \textit{Ras} (mutated to oncogene form in 25\% of cancers)
	\begin{itemize}
        \item Intracellular GTP-binding protein in mitogen signaling cascades
        \item Oncogene form has \textbf{lost} GTPase function necessary to deactivate itself
    \end{itemize}
    \item Cancer cells \textbf{can become resistant} through mutation of the target itself or through mutations that activate a different pathway.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Side Effects of Targeted Cancer Therapies}
It was expected that targeted cancer therapies would be less toxic than traditional chemotherapy, but substantial side effects do occur.
\begin{itemize}
	\item The most common are diarrhea and liver problems (hepatitis, elevated liver enzymes)
\end{itemize}
Also,
\begin{itemize}
	\item skin problems
    \item ail changes
    \item hair depigmentation
    \item deficiencies in blood clotting
    \item deficiencies in wound healing
    \item gastrointestinal perforation
\end{itemize}
The few targeted therapies approved for use in children can have different side effects than in adults, including:
\begin{itemize}
	\item immunosuppression
    \item impaired sperm production
\end{itemize}
Common theme?  Effects on \textbf{normal} proliferating cell lineages.

\subsection*{CAR-T Cell Therapy Procedure}
Adulr self-renewing T-lymphocytes are genetically modified to increase immune potency against specific cancer cells
\begin{itemize}
	\item T-cells are obtained from the patient
	\item The T-cells are genetically modified in vitro to express a synthetic Chimeric Antigen Receptor gene (transduction with \textbf{lentivirus vectors})
	\item The modified T-cells are re-introduced into patient to attach cancer cells
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Severe Risks with CAR-T Therapies}
Example: KYMRIAH
\begin{itemize}
	\item Cytokine release syndrome (CRS)
	\begin{itemize}
        \item 74\% of patients within 10 days
        \item 3\% deaths within 30 days
    \end{itemize}
	\item Neurological toxicities: 
    \begin{itemize}
        \item 58\% of patients within 8 weeks
        \item Headache, encephalopathy, delirium, anxiety, sleep disorders, tremor, peripheral neuropathy, seizures, mutism, aphasia
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Limitations of CAR-T}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Highly specific antigens are not available for most cancers
	\item Possible approach:
	\begin{itemize}
        \item ``Double-gated'' CAR-T
        \item Engineer CAR-T cells so that activation requires simultaneous recognition of two different antigens that are only co-expressed in the tumor cells
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
